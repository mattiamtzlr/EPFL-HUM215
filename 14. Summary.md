- **Divine Authority and Cosmic Order**:
    - **Egypt**: Gods maintained _ma'at_ (cosmic order); Pharaohs were seen as divine figures ensuring stability and harmony.
    - **Greece**: Gods were anthropomorphic, reflecting human traits; divine authority was less absolute, with gods engaging in conflicts.
    - **Near East**: Gods represented cosmic order through dominance and warfare; kings acted as intermediaries between gods and people.

<br>

- **Human-Divine Relationship**:
    - **Egypt**: Humans interacted with gods through rituals to ensure favor and life continuity; Pharaohs were key mediators.
    - **Greece**: Gods were human-like and capricious; people had personal relationships with gods, marked by offerings and prayers.
    - **Near East**: Religious practices were formalized, with kings and priests mediating to gain divine favor for societal survival.

<br>

- **Role in Governance and Society**:    
    - **Egypt**: Gods legitimized royal power; Pharaohs embodied divine rule, with religious practices supporting governance and social stability.
    - **Greece**: Gods influenced culture and ethics but did not directly legitimize political authority.
    - **Near East**: Kings were seen as chosen by the gods; deities were associated with city-states, reinforcing royal power.

<br>

- **Pantheons as Reflections of Cultural Values**:    
    - **Egypt**: Focused on stability, tradition, and continuity; gods were protectors of life and afterlife.
    - **Greece**: Emphasized individuality, human nature, and moral ambiguity; gods were relatable but flawed.
